# Ordered Quaternionic State Map

## Why this matters
One of the strongest current structural moves in Khantraction is to stop thinking only in raw quaternion components and instead use an ordered quaternionic state map:

$
Q(\omega,\theta,\phi,\rho)=e^{\omega}e^{\theta i}e^{\phi j}e^{\rho k}.
$

This should **not** be read as a naive commuting identity for

$
e^{\omega+\theta i+\phi j+\rho k},
$

because quaternion units do not commute. It is better interpreted as an **ordered factorized quaternionic state construction**.

That matters because it gives Khantraction:
- one clean scale coordinate,
- three ordered internal angles,
- and a natural differential geometry on internal state space.

## Component expansion
Using

$
e^{\theta i}=\cos\theta+i\sin\theta,
\qquad
e^{\phi j}=\cos\phi+j\sin\phi,
\qquad
e^{\rho k}=\cos\rho+k\sin\rho,
$

and the quaternion multiplication rules

$
ij=k,
\qquad
jk=i,
\qquad
ki=j,
$

with reversed order giving a minus sign, one finds

$
Q=e^{\omega}(a+bi+cj+dk),
$

where

$
a=\cos\theta\cos\phi\cos\rho-\sin\theta\sin\phi\sin\rho,
$

$
b=\sin\theta\cos\phi\cos\rho+\cos\theta\sin\phi\sin\rho,
$

$
c=\cos\theta\sin\phi\cos\rho-\sin\theta\cos\phi\sin\rho,
$

$
d=\cos\theta\cos\phi\sin\rho+\sin\theta\sin\phi\cos\rho.
$

So the ordered state map is an explicit nonlinear coordinate map from

$
(\omega,\theta,\phi,\rho)
$

to quaternion components

$
(x_0,x_1,x_2,x_3)=(e^{\omega}a,e^{\omega}b,e^{\omega}c,e^{\omega}d).
$

## Differential geometry: the Maurer–Cartan form
The next important step is not just the Jacobian but the left Maurer–Cartan form

$
Q^{-1}dQ.
$

For the ordered product above, this becomes

$
Q^{-1}dQ = d\omega + i\,\sigma_1 + j\,\sigma_2 + k\,\sigma_3,
$

with

$
\sigma_1 = \cos 2\phi\cos 2\rho\, d\theta - \sin 2\rho\, d\phi,
$

$
\sigma_2 = \cos 2\phi\sin 2\rho\, d\theta + \cos 2\rho\, d\phi,
$

$
\sigma_3 = \sin 2\phi\, d\theta + d\rho.
$

This is the first clean geometric decomposition of the ordered-state manifold.

It shows that:
- $d\omega$ is a pure scale channel,
- the angular variables mix nontrivially into the three imaginary directions,
- and the mixing is generated by the noncommutative ordering itself.

## Induced metric in ordered-state space
A natural quadratic form comes from the quaternion norm of the Maurer–Cartan form:

$
|Q^{-1}dQ|^2 = d\omega^2 + \sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2 + \sigma_3^2.
$

After simplification this becomes

$
|Q^{-1}dQ|^2
= d\omega^2 + d\theta^2 + d\phi^2 + d\rho^2 + 2\sin 2\phi\, d\theta d\rho.
$

So in the coordinate basis

$
(d\omega,d\theta,d\phi,d\rho),
$

the induced metric is

$
G=
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\
0 & 1 & 0 & \sin 2\phi \\
0 & 0 & 1 & 0 \\
0 & \sin 2\phi & 0 & 1
\end{pmatrix}.
$

This has several immediate consequences:
- the scale coordinate $\omega$ decouples cleanly from the angular sector,
- the middle angle $\phi$ controls the mixing between the $\theta$ and $\rho$ directions,
- and the internal angular geometry can become strongly anisotropic when $|\sin 2\phi|$ is large.

## Why this is interesting for Khantraction
This makes the ordered-state map more than a convenient refit of quaternion components.

It gives a plausible internal geometry for the branch family:
- one scale-like direction,
- three ordered angular channels,
- and a built-in mechanism for internal channel mixing and softness.

That matters because the current quaternion-rich family already appears to occupy a dominant locked internal-angle sector. The ordered-state geometry now shows how such locking and anisotropy could be structurally meaningful rather than just numerically cosmetic.

## Current status
At present, the ordered-state map is best treated as a **promising internal coordinate geometry** for Khantraction, not yet as the full physical dynamics. The clean next question is whether the observed soft branch directions and rich-regime locking are mostly explained by this intrinsic geometry or require an additional branch-dependent dynamical stiffness operator.
